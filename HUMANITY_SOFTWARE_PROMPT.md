# Humanity Software Prompt

Use this prompt when brainstorming portfolio projects that solve real human problems. The goal is not to make a list of impressive apps. The goal is to find neglected workflows where careful software can increase human capability, safety, dignity, access, coordination, learning, repair, resilience, or trust.

## Core Prompt

Brainstorm software projects that solve humanity-level problems, especially where current tools are missing, expensive, too online, too complex, too centralized, not accessible, or not built for people with low resources. For each idea, identify the user, the painful workflow, why the market underbuilds it, the smallest useful MVP, the safety limits, the data model, and how it could become a credible portfolio project.

Prioritize ideas that:

- Help people survive, learn, repair, coordinate, communicate, understand rights, access services, preserve knowledge, or make better decisions.
- Work in low-bandwidth, offline, local-first, old-device, multilingual, or crisis conditions.
- Serve fragmented users who are rarely attractive to venture-backed software.
- Can start as a small static app, PWA, CLI, local database, browser extension, SMS workflow, printable kit, or open data tool.
- Have clear technical depth: data modelling, search, maps, sync, export, accessibility, testing, privacy, AI assistance, visualization, or workflow automation.
- Are honest about uncertainty, especially in health, legal, safety, disaster, education, finance, and public-benefit contexts.

## Missing Context To Include

### 1. Who Is Usually Left Out

Look for people whose needs are important but commercially awkward:

- Rural clinics, small schools, community groups, repair shops, libraries, local governments, shelters, churches, cooperatives, field workers, informal workers, elders, disabled users, caregivers, migrants, refugees, tenants, small farmers, low-income households, and people without stable internet.
- Volunteers and coordinators who currently use paper, chat groups, memory, or spreadsheets.
- People who need plain-language instructions rather than dashboards built for specialists.

### 2. Real-World Constraints

Every strong idea should name its constraints:

- Offline use.
- Low-end Android phones.
- Shared devices.
- Weak power.
- Unreliable internet.
- Multilingual users.
- Low literacy or high stress.
- Print handoff.
- Local privacy risk.
- Stale or uncertain information.
- Need for exports that survive vendor shutdown.

### 3. Safety And Trust

Trustworthy public-good software should:

- State what it cannot do.
- Show source dates, review status, and uncertainty.
- Avoid pretending to be a doctor, lawyer, emergency service, eligibility worker, engineer, or public authority.
- Keep sensitive data local by default.
- Make stale data visible.
- Provide printable and exportable outputs.
- Prefer checklists, source-backed guidance, and referral paths over confident advice.

### 4. Technical Signals

A portfolio idea is stronger when it can prove engineering range:

- Local-first architecture with IndexedDB, SQLite, or file-based storage.
- Offline PWA with service worker caching.
- Structured content schema.
- Search and filtering.
- Import/export with JSON, CSV, Markdown, PDF, or GeoJSON.
- Accessibility-first UI.
- Internationalization.
- Map, timeline, graph, or decision-tree visualization.
- Audit trails for sensitive actions.
- Testable validation rules.
- AI assistance with citations and human review.

### 5. MVP Shape

Good first versions should be small but useful:

- A checklist generator.
- A field guide.
- A local registry.
- A printable action plan.
- A decision tree.
- A map notebook.
- A lightweight inventory.
- A document pack builder.
- A translation card generator.
- A queue, routing, or triage board.

Avoid starting with accounts, payments, social feeds, complex marketplaces, or heavy backend infrastructure unless the problem truly needs them.

## Evaluation Questions

Use these questions to choose what to build first:

1. Who is the first real user?
2. What do they do today without this tool?
3. What is the painful moment the software improves?
4. Can the MVP help one person or group without a large institution?
5. Can it run offline or degrade gracefully?
6. What data must be private?
7. What data needs review, citations, or expiry dates?
8. What false claim would be dangerous?
9. What can be printed or exported?
10. What technical skill does the project prove?
11. What public artifact can be shown in a portfolio?
12. What would make it useful after the demo?

## Output Format

For each idea, write:

- Name.
- User.
- Problem.
- Why neglected.
- MVP.
- Safety boundaries.
- Technical signal.
- Portfolio artifact.

## Strong Prompt Variant

Generate 100 software project ideas that solve real human problems. Bias toward neglected, underfunded, low-resource, offline-first, accessibility, resilience, public-health, education, civic, environmental, repair, and coordination needs. For each idea, describe the user, the problem, why existing software fails them, a small MVP, safety limits, and the technical signal it would demonstrate in a portfolio. Avoid vague charity language. Prefer concrete workflows that can become working apps, PWAs, CLIs, browser extensions, local-first tools, open data dashboards, field guides, maps, or printable kits. Include ideas for people with weak internet, shared devices, low literacy, disability, language barriers, local privacy risks, and crisis conditions.
